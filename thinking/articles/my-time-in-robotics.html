My Time in Robotics

Five years, three programs, thousands of unforgettable experiences

Brandon Gong
August 3rd, 2020

The two robots built during my time on my school's FRC team.

If, someday a few decades into the future, I forget all else about my time in grade school — all the friends I made, teachers I had, and classes I took — there is one part of high school that I will never forget: my time in robotics. From 7th to 11th grade, and through FTC, FRC, and VEX, school robotics programs have given me countless experiences and learning opportunities that have made me who I am today.

I wanted to recount a year-by-year history of my time in robotics, starting with the humble beginnings: 2015.

7th Grade

7th grade was my first year doing robotics. It was also the first year of the team's — FTC Team 10034 — existence. As a result, we experienced many technical difficulties, as every error or challenge in the process was new to us. I remember it taking several weeks to even figure out how to properly wire up all of the components, realizing that unlike other more basic robot kits such as those made by LEGO, wires for data and power were separate. It took a few weeks more to then get a motor to turn. Everything was new, and despite the best intentions of some of the parents in trying to help out, we frequently found ourselves stuck in a dead end, unsure how to proceed.

For me, there was an added challenge; I was younger than everyone else on the team by one grade, and furthermore my new teammates had all been former teammates on an FLL team the year before. As a result, I was the stranger, and the rest of my team were friends with each other. Thus, I also had to work on assimilating into the team and getting a feel for their personalities and how they worked together.

And I had to do this as an anti-social kid, who didn't even have as many friends as there were kids on the team.

Heading into the robotics team, I expected to build really cool, advanced robots with unique designs and efficient functions (I remember proposing a design for that year's game with the robot just being a cube with legs that could protrude out of it from different sides, theoretically enabling it to "walk" and also "climb" up a set of bars). Very quickly, however, I realized that my expectations were extremely unrealistic.

Not only did I learn for the first time what it meant to work as a team, I also learned to celebrate little victories. A turning wheel meant a successful day; code deploying without issues was cause for celebration. In the end, the memories that stick with me are not of the end result (in fact, I don't even remember what the robot looked like); I remember the long days spent in that lab, a plate of room-temperature pizza next to me, puzzling out random problems and errors with the rest of the team. That experience, to me, was the most valuable.

8th Grade

I rejoined Team 10034 for my 8th grade year, but the rest of my old teammates had graduated from middle school, leaving me once again in a situation where I had to integrate into a new team with new people. This time, however, I was the captain; in just my second year of doing robotics ever, I was already faced with the challenge of teaching and coordinating other team members.

My role had shifted, and knowledge and experience had carried over from last year, but the cherished feeling of working on the robot, seeing it evolve and grow over time, hitting issue after issue. Our robot design began to improve significantly; we incorporated plexiglass boards and more creative mechanism designs, taking better advantage of size and space constraints.

We also hit many new and unexpected issues; the code and setup had changed somewhat from the previous year, so there was new workflows to figure out; our former mentor and teacher had moved away, leaving us again trying to solve many new challenges on our own.

I pulled an all-nighter the night before the competition, fixing the code and trying to add new functionality. The robot came out that night looking completely different from before, with parts hastily cut from styrofoam board and rubber bands. There were still countless issues and instabilities, but we were out of time.

We finished off the season with our best placement yet. Though it's not too impressive, third place at our regional competition was a huge success for our second-year team with only one veteran member, competing against teams that have been around for years with mentors and members with many seasons of experience under their belt.

9th Grade

My freshman year of high school, I had left the FTC team that I had helped start behind and joined the high school's FRC team, Team 5002. Once again in a new environment and working with completely different hardware and software, I had to adapt. I had some of my former teammates who had also moved on to this new team, but there were still many that I had never met. And as one of the three freshmen on a large team of mostly juniors and seniors, the age gap felt as wide as it had ever been.

I also faced conflict with the lead programmer at the time. He and I both had significantly more experience than other students in code, and, like matching poles on a magnet, we repelled. We frequently argued, shut down each other's ideas, and generally disliked each other. I took every moment to try to prove my skill and competence, and to him I must have been the annoying overconfident freshman, intruding into the team that he had been on for so many years.

Over time, as we continued to work together, though, we began to understand each other more. We each recognized the abilities of the other, and to this day I still believe he is the most talented programmer I have ever worked with. I closely shadowed him while he worked on the robot, and I learned as much as I could.

Together, we brought major changes to our team; we translated our entire codebase to Python from Java, making the code easier for even members of the build team to understand, and making the programming process much faster. We also made heavier use of version control, dividing work up into branches for everyone to contribute.

Finally, we programmed a swerve drive base, one of the most complex drivetrains — using eight motors — to build, maintain, and program.

This year was perhaps my most instructive year in programming, especially in terms of working with other programmers.

10th Grade

With the former lead programmer graduating from high school, I was left with the most experience. As a result, in only my second year of FRC, I became the lead programmer. I was determined to continue the path of improvement that we had started last year. I believed Python really enabled a lot for the team, so I decided to continue using Python for this season.
