Why I Love Piano

I'm not very good, but I still play every day.

Brandon Gong
August 30th, 2020

(This essay was written as a warm-up for college application writing.)

Some artists prefer the fine-lined, delicate precision of a pencil. Others favor the weight of a soft-bristled brush, making bold, vibrant sweeps across the canvas. For me, it is an endless expanse of white and black keys: mountains as black as obsidian standing tall and proud in a sea of ivory white. From these keys and my fingers alone, I build new worlds for myself, each brimming with unique colors, textures, sights, and sounds, away from the constant cacophony and tension that plagues our daily lives.

Just as there is a sharp contrast between the dark and light keys of the keyboard, playing piano engenders a myriad of oxymorons:

The keys are regular, rectangular, and discrete, yet the music flows out of them, amorphous and continually shifting and changing yet ever perfect like a droplet of water falling from the pointed tip of a green leaf.

My fingers are mindless tools that follow my control, yet over the piano they leap and run about with a mind of their own, each one with its own voice and purpose within the piece as a whole.

In school and extracurriculars, I focus on mastering concrete subjects — physics, biology, chemistry, calculus, engineering, subjects that I can attack with my familiar toolbox of formulas, logic, and a pencil — yet at my piano I free myself from the meticulous study of the strict and orderly and swoop, glide, and soar unbounded with wings of creativity and abstract beauty.

The world around me is chaotic, angry, and out of my control, yet within myself I am calm and joyful, reveling in the bright, warm rays of the music, pulling and pushing at the direction of the melodies and harmonies at will like a potter shaping a lump of clay on his wheel into a beautiful ceramic dish.

Unlike other artists, my work will never be prominently displayed in a museum, or auctioned off at exorbitant prices; I will never be praised or immortalized for my technique or skill; I will never tackle the grand, thunderous works of Tchaikovsky and Rachmaninoff or the finger-twisting blind jumps and tricky rhythms of Liszt and Bartok on a concert stage before a live audience — no, I am the conductor; I am his orchestra; I am their instruments; I am the sole listener. And I am fine with this. Piano to me serves as a reminder that I cannot be and should not strive to be the best in everything that I do. It serves as a reminder to slow down, to enjoy and appreciate the beautiful and intricate details of life, to reconnect with the familiar and discover something new.

At the end of my practice, when my fingers have struck the final chord, when I release the last resonating notes with the lift of a pedal, when I close my notes with a satisfying thud and turn off the practice lamp with a final click, I return to my study desk — to the code I was writing, the equation I was solving — but the music has not left me. The energy and exhilaration of playing still flow through my fingers; the sounds and melodies still flow through my mind. I feel refreshed and revitalized as I begin my work anew.
